Legend has established her name as Brown Beauty. Puritans, Congregationalists and Unitarians did not name their objects. Doing so bordered on idolatry. Paul Revere gave three depositions of his midnight ride. He spoke kindly of the mare of thirteen hands ridden that night. Yet, he never mentioned her name. Samuel Larkin owned the horse and Deacon John Larkin made it available for Paul Revere that night. Samuel's farm, the mare's home, was burned down by the British on June 17, 1775, during the battle of Breed's Hill. The good deacon moved eighty miles away absent one wonderful mare. This ode describes the events of that night through the eyes of that wonderful mare, lost somewhere between Lincoln and Charlestown. </div> <div class="poem-content"> ----------Ode To Brown Beauty, If That Be Her Name------------ The barn my home Me, just another farm genome Hybridized to New England’s natural brome A whistle, a gesture, a clamor, Barely an accolade That is how I came No congregationalist glorified my name Three years beyond birth on Larkin’s Farm Manicured, washed scrubbed and tethered No plow in my life, ever A chaise, far from my hindquarters, I lived to race, no iron shoes, The best of hay To augment my DNA The thoroughbreds disturbed day and night In Spring spawned by the pink moonlight Sheltered I, by the Deacon this season Preserved for tonight April 18, 1775, my time had come Though small I will lose to none Thirty riders all new to me A minion worshiped the belfry All leaped when the lanterns displayed One minute two lanterns Newman arrayed At once the race began But wait, No starting line No finish All riders gone In a minute No congrats, no porridge, no winner declared I sat, then sulked My delay soon clear I heard wooden oars Against the harbors rising tide North From Boston's Back Bay Surged Bentley, Richardson and Revere A determined man sprange through the waves Deacon Larkin directed him to me I be damned! A new rider Lent I this evening to a swarthy man Upon my Narragansett breath This Calvinist, I soon would not forget His ancestry ran back to France HIs mastery to the hammer and chisel But we just met! He stood five-foot eight Me? A slighted 13 hands The moon, six degrees behind Cast his giant silhouette For certain he’d have too firm a grip Neighhhh, no sway belayed No imbalance from his girth His knees firm on my ribs Spurs not once engaged Reigns released I chose the gait! So pleased I had his faith Galloped north to the Mystic path He would have bridled just the same Not a single word! On a mare, in Spring, he knew best! His metered breath fell on my mane Like a metronome, he synchronized the pace We rode six miles, came face to face Two Lobster-backs in Medford place I would not subvert by rearing back Those large English ponies proud to parade The first pranced high on Mystic ice Horseshoes betrayed a grip as metal might Into the spring mud, not seen again that night His stablemate, too heavy, too old, chose to abdicate I picked up speed to Lexington Green Greeted and treated as if I carried the day Some water, but not a moment to graze To the yellow house, he pointed, I aimed Met Sergeant Munroe, his fidelity in vain “You’ll have noise soon enough,” Mr. Revere exclaimed Two men, two women, nine militiamen apprised Dawes, his horse, Paul and I, Rode to finish our rebel ride At Lincoln town, a doctor, we met in stride Prescott's thoroughbred made great time Showed much too much interest in mine Restless as his rider for relief Two similar yearnings One man, one breed On a medical mission he exclaimed! Likely a diversion to shield a Lexington dame? Confirmed, all Sons of Liberty, A Concordian merely twenty-five We moved as a group with Dawes a heavy ride Three men, three horse, in single file Surrounded in Lincoln by the King's officers Twelve from their ambush in scarlet coatee, White pants, leather gaiters Silver pistols gleaming Their swords unsheathing The Major with tunic asked our loyalty A moment reprieve for me I smelled the young grass I saw the fresh flowers And heard the rush of water four beats away Dawes and Prescott made good their escape Over the wall, Down the dark road, each echoed “Put on, Put on,” Saw their surtouts fade as they begone I was distracted My dear huguenot and I, Far too exposed No less, a sergeant, an ensign, a Lieutenant, A quarrelsome major blocked the Bay Path Arrogant orders from beyond the pond “These Rebels need to bow to the crown” A pistol thrust to Mr. Revere’s temple Major Mitchell demanded to what matters Facing death, no weapons for defense, He turned to what he had left, A distraction, a hesitation, a Major diversion My lips snickered with pride at his inversion “Dear Major, fire away Bring on 500 hundred men from Lexington way” The good major, his conduct broke, or duty called? Paul ordered to dismount On came a heavy redcoat remount We hastened east to safety in numbers To warn the regiment of their peril My master left with little recourse, Walked back the way he came Not done yet! A third act remains Hancock & Adams warned at midnight They lingered till first light I stood facing farmers in Lexington green Mr. Hancock, he wished to fight Now Revere, Dawes and Adams fed his pride “On to Philadelphia. John, your history is incomplete! Direct that charge, confront that King” John, concerned with his enormous trunk Stored at the Buckman on the green Inside to entertain wife and Aunt a cooked salmon, And two seditious scrolls 500 Sons of Liberty, 14,000 militiamen enrolled There he was, Revere appeared He dragged the trunk 700 Brown Bess in his rear A first shot on Lexington green, Many more, a charge, then shouts, A pursuit to Concord, Then a turn-about Fifteen hours now, No fresh hay, no water to spare To Menotomy the sarge rode rough Enough! Untested, exposed, this heavyweight in red! My stamina drained Randomly rushing about I bucked, plunged my mane, kicked dust throughout Luckily he had no spurs, In lieu, his shoes hobnobbed my loins, Both boots abused He soon found his best One fist punched my jowls The other my bridle A waste of time, there would be no revival Musket balls rattled Two cannon exploded so near Mane singed, eyes blind, lung concussed Beyond my wind The Lobster-back, he lost his grip My load refined, A brief respite I knew no way home New to me this Old Bay path Hills to my right Houses inflamed Trespassed, ransacked Four legs confused Stiffened my gait Stumbled to my knees Inhaled white smoke Heaved what was left Bloody hands on my reigns, Desperates clung to my tail Nothing left, Approached the moment of death Alas Menotomy Square I smelled the water A trough was near I reared to clear the path Desperate hands reach up in despair I bolted, Opposed by bayonets I slipped, a prick Neighed in fear I turned to those in my rear A hand on each bridle, and on my halter My tongue compressed, lips plastered Two teeth unhitched Five tonsils swelled Can’t swallow, can’t breath Forced back on my quarters, Can’t rise, though ordered I smelled the saltpeter A spark, white smoke, foul taste Dislodged more teeth, Lost vision, numb, gone Can’t hear, nor feel They come again I look to the rear For that man Revere dreaming of one more ride His balance His enveloping hands, Those guiding knees His breath on my mane I hope I pleased We worked so well, So fast, so urgently He knew me as a mare of 13 hands An American hybrid to forage in snow, Ice, mud and granite on any road Able in a day to reach any borough No flu, stomach woes, or arthritic legs Immune to New England's maladies Outclassed those English ponies on this special day At my peak, my time transpired My breed displayed its best Morgan or Narragansett no need to guess Eye to eye though small indeed We interbred to please If given time to leave the next of me My genome would be complete Left with one more regret He knew not my name Thirty years aside Mr. Belknap penned our ride My epitaph, my legacy A mare with no name. </div> <div class="footer"> The End by Mitch
